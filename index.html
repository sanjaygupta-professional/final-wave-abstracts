Who Owns the Ground Truth?

The Verification Layer and the Quiet Capture of Insurance and Land Rights

Every institution that manages physical risk rests on a quiet assumption: that knowledge of the ground's state over time is scarce and contestable. A decade of daily whole-Earth imaging, absorbed into foundation models, is deleting that scarcity — turning the planet's past into a queryable, retroactive record. This chapter argues the consequence is not efficiency but capture. Insurance and land rights do not collapse — capital still absorbs catastrophe, the state still enforces title — but their verification layer, the trust function that says what is true about the ground, relocates to whoever owns the record: the observation monopoly becomes de facto insurer-of-record and registry-of-record at once. The chapter architects four dated trajectories to 2045 — institutional inertia, data feudalism, planetary commons, observation wars — and takes India as the proof-case: the one state with the digital-public-infrastructure muscle (Aadhaar, SVAMITVA, PMFBY) to choose commons over feudalism, and the place the fork is decided first. As a remedy, it proposes the planetary record be constituted as sovereign digital public infrastructure before ownership hardens. Its novelty lies in the unstudied convergence of retroactive planetary observation, institutional trust functions, and ownership-of-record. Falsifiable 2035/2045 anchors, with disconfirming conditions, are stated in-chapter.

Old Before Rich

India and the First AI-Mediated Aging Transition

Every economics of aging rests on a quiet assumption: that societies grow rich before they grow old, so accumulated wealth pays for the machines and the care. India will break that assumption. Its demographic dividend closes around the 2050s — decades before it reaches high income — making it the first large civilization to attempt an aging transition before wealth, and to attempt it on AI: automation deployed as a simultaneous hedge against two shortages at once, a shrinking young workforce and a caregiver deficit measured in millions. The evidence that automation answers aging comes only from rich agers — Japan, Germany, Korea (Acemoglu & Restrepo); China, where the AI-against-the-demographic-trap argument is already mainstream, ages roughly three times richer per head. India crosses the boundary in reverse. Whether that broadens opportunity or hardens into a two-tier society — an AI-served elite above an excluded rural majority, and women whose unpaid care AI displaces without alternative — is decided by design, not by the technology's arrival. The chapter architects four dated trajectories (inertia, two-tier capture, designed inclusion, substitution overshoot), argues India's scale and cost ceiling force substitution — contesting the practitioner consensus (TCS) of AI-as-supplement — and proposes care as public infrastructure as the designed-inclusion path. Its novelty lies in the unstudied convergence of pre-rich aging, dual-hedge automation, and distributional design. Falsifiable 2035/2050 anchors, with disconfirming conditions, are stated in-chapter.
